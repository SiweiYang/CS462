Lecture Summaries -- Winter 2011

Week 1

Lecture 1: Wednesday, January 5, 2011:
I handed out the Course information sheet. Problem Set 1 is available and due January 12. Slide: formal languages and connections to other areas. We went over the basic definitions of concepts such as word (string), language, reversal, power, palindrome, empty string, prefix, suffix, subword, etc. (pages 1-4 of the course textbook). We also went over the definitions in Sections 2.1 and 2.2. We proved the first theorem of Lyndon-Schützenberger (Theorem 2.3.2).

Week 2

Lecture 2: Monday, January 10, 2011:
Second theorem of Lyndon-Schützenberger (Theorem 2.3.3). Primitive words. Theorem 2.3.4. Introduction to a version of the Fine-Wilf theorem (Theorem 2.3.5). Open problem #1: is the set of primitive words over {0, 1} context-free?

Lecture 3: Wednesday, January 12, 2011:
Problem Set 2 is available and due January 19. Proof of Theorem 2.3.5. Theorem 2.3.6. Definitions of conjugates and bordered words. Theorems 2.4.2 and 2.4.3.

Week 3

Lecture 4: Monday, January 17, 2011:
Thm. 2.4.5. The definition of square, overlap, etc., and the Thue-Morse sequence. Introduction to applications of the Thue-Morse sequence: chess, music, etc. For more about the work of Per Nørgård, see here.

Lecture 5: Wednesday, January 19, 2011:
Theorems 2.5.1 and 2.5.2, and the rest of the material in section 2.6. Problem Set 3 is available and due January 26. We mentioned two open problems: Problem 2 and Problem 3.

For more about the Thue-Morse sequence, see this survey paper.

Week 4

Lecture 6: Monday, January 24, 2011:
Languages. Definition of union, concatenation, star, reverse, etc. Regular languages. Review of what we know about them. Outline of what we will be doing over the next 6 lectures. Quotient: definition, examples. Proof of Theorem 3.2.3. Morphisms. Substitutions. Examples. Proof of Theorem 3.3.5. Definition of inverse morphism.

Lecture 7: Wednesday, January 26 2011:
Problem Set 4 is now available.

We continued our discussion of inverse morphism. We showed how to represent shuffle using morphisms and inverse morphisms (Example 3.3.8), and proved Theorem 3.3.9. We covered the material in section 3.4. We discussed automata with output (section 3.1), and began section 3.5.

Week 5

Lecture 8: Monday, January 31, 2011:
Transducers. Example: transducer for expressing base-3 numbers as sums of 2 Cantor numbers. Nivat's theorem. Example: removing leading zeroes. Introduction to 2DFA's.

Lecture 9: Wednesday, February 2 2011:
Problem Set 5 is now available. Don't forget, solutions to the problem sets are available on the course home page.

2DFA's: formal definition. Examples. Proof that 2DFA's accept the same class of languages as the DFA's. Example of the conversion. The transformation automaton. Theorem 3.7.2, Corollary 3.7.3, Example 3.7.5.

Automata and Boolean matrices. Introduction to the Myhill-Nerode theorem.

Lecture 11: Wednesday, February 9 2011:
More on the Myhill-Nerode theorem. Proof. Examples. Application to NFA to DFA conversion. Introduction to DFA minimization.

Week 7

Lecture 12: Monday, February 14, 2011:
Problem Set 7 is now available.

We proved that the minimization algorithm works. Applications: shortest string distinguishing two automata. Brzozowski's algorithm (introduction).

Lecture 13: Wednesday, February 16, 2011:
Solutions to Problem Set 6 now available.

We proved that Brzozowski's algorithm works. Example of Brzozowski's algorithm. The operations sup() and sub(). Minimal elements. Proof that sub(L) and sup(L) are always regular.

You may want to read this paper which gives the proof that minimal elements of the prime numbers (expressed in base 10) are as described on the cards I handed out. It also gives an idea of how to approach the problem on the problem set (but the one on the problem set is much easier).

Week 8

Lecture 14: Monday, February 28, 2011: We looked at state complexity, proved the state complexity of intersection, and nondeterministic state complexity. I mentioned an open problem: the state complexity of (c(L*))*. Here by c(A) I mean the complement of A.

Lecture 15: Wednesday, March 2 2011: Problem Set 8 is available.

Introduction to context-free languages. Closure under morphism (easy); inverse morphism (harder). Every unary-context-free language is regular. Intro to Ogden's lemma. (You can use Theorem 4.1.6 in Problem set 8 even though we didn't do it yet.)

Week 9

Lecture 16: Monday, March 7 2011: Ogden's lemma. Review of the ordinary pumping lemma. Proof of Ogden's lemma. Example of how to apply it (the n! trick). Application: inherently ambiguous languages.

Lecture 17: Wednesday, March 9 2011: Parikh's theorem. Problem Set 9 is available; due March 16.

Prof. Shallit will be unable to hold regularly-scheduled office hours on Thursday, March 10.

Week 10

Lecture 18: Monday, March 14 2011: Proof of non-closure of CFL's under quotient. Deterministic context-free languages. Closure of class of DCFL's under complement. DCFL's and Myhill-Nerode classes. Introduction to recognition and parsing algorithms.

Lecture 19: Wednesday, March 16 2011: The CYK algorithm. Using the CYK algorithm to generate a parse tree. Introduction to LR parsing. The Knuth automaton.

Open problem: given CFL's L1 and L2 with L1 ⊆ L2 and L2 - L1 infinite, is there always a CFL L such that L1 ⊆ L ⊆ L2 such that L2 - L and L - L1 are both infinite? This is not known, even in the case where L2 = Σ*.
